US healthcare clients under compliance pressure cannot afford ambiguous requirements. For over two years I served as the primary BA and delivery owner for Aetna and Humana — eliciting, documenting, and validating requirements with enough precision that development teams could build without returning for a single clarifying question.

The Environment

Regulated delivery. High stakes. No margin for vagueness.

At Cotiviti India, I was the primary Business Analyst serving two of North America's largest health insurance organisations. This was not exploratory analytics or internal reporting. It was structured, regulated delivery — where every requirement had to be traceable, every data output had to meet compliance standards, and every gap between what the business needed and what the development team built had a measurable financial cost.

This is the environment that shaped how I think about requirements — not as documents to be produced, but as contracts between business intent and technical delivery. A vague requirement is not a minor inefficiency. In a regulated environment, it is a guaranteed rework cycle measured in compliance risk.

"A requirement is only complete when a developer can build from it without asking a single clarifying question."

Requirement Layers — My Standard Format
Business Requirement — What & Why
The analytics system shall provide performance monitoring reports segmented by processing category, updated on a defined refresh cycle, and accessible to authorised users — to support operational decision-making and regulatory reporting obligations.
Functional Requirement — What It Must Do
The reporting module shall calculate and display category-level KPIs including volume, variance vs. benchmark, and trend direction — with drill-down to individual record level. Data shall refresh within the agreed SLA. User access shall be role-controlled.
Acceptance Criteria — How We Know It Is Done
Report loads within performance threshold. All KPI calculations match validated benchmark logic. Drill-down returns correct record-level detail. Role permissions are enforced. Data refresh timestamp visible on all views. QA sign-off required before release.

* Illustrative format — actual requirements protected by NDA

The Work

What two years of enterprise BA delivery looks like.

Stakeholder Engagement

Structured sessions with US client teams

Regular engagement with US-based business stakeholders — eliciting needs, clarifying edge cases, managing scope refinement across multiple concurrent workstreams. Every requirement had an owner and a sign-off date.

Gap Analysis

Current state vs. future state

Systematic comparison of existing processes against business objectives — identifying missing logic, data gaps, and process inefficiencies before they became development problems.

Root Cause Analysis

Why it happened — not just what happened

When delivery anomalies surfaced, I applied structured RCA to distinguish systemic issues from one-time events. Every finding fed directly into continuous improvement cycles.

Data Governance

Compliance built in from line one

Healthcare data carries strict compliance obligations. Security configurations and access controls were not afterthoughts — they were written into the requirement from the start.

The Principle That Governs This Work
Regulated environments make good BAs.
When the cost of a vague requirement is measured in compliance risk and client contract exposure — you stop tolerating vagueness. Two years in US regulated healthcare delivery produce a BA who writes requirements that build correctly the first time. That standard does not leave when the domain changes. It transfers directly to government IT delivery.
Why This Matters for SNB

Healthcare compliance. Government compliance. The discipline is identical.

SNB's Salesforce portfolio serves citizen-facing platforms where imprecise requirements have real consequences — delayed services, rework cycles, budget overruns. The BA discipline I developed in US regulated healthcare transfers directly. Different domain. Same non-negotiable standard.
